Dose of Clarity Review

Wilms' Tumor
(Nephroblastoma)

"You only need to hear it once."


DO NOT PALPATE!

Never palpate the abdomen of a child with suspected or known Wilms' Tumor.

WHY?

Palpation may rupture the tumor capsule, leading to tumor cell seeding (spreading cancer to the peritoneum).

What is Wilms' Tumor?

Wilms' Tumor (Nephroblastoma) is a malignant renal tumor (cancer of the kidney) that arises from embryonic kidney cells (metanephric blastema).

It is the most common malignant kidney tumor in children, usually diagnosed before age 5 (Peak: 2-4 years).

Etiology & Risk Factors

Genetics

Mutations in the WT1 or WT2 gene on chromosome 11. Can be Autosomal dominant.

Demographics

Slightly more common in females and more frequent in African-American children.

Congenital Anomalies & Syndromes

  • Aniridia: Absence of the iris in the eye.
  • Hemihypertrophy: One side of the body is larger than the other.
  • Syndromes: Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome, Denys-Drash syndrome.

Pathophysiology

Click through the steps to see how a Nephroblastoma develops:

1

Fetal Development Failure

During fetal development, some kidney cells (metanephric blastema) fail to mature properly into normal kidney tissue.

2

Uncontrollable Division

After birth, instead of maturing, these embryonic cells continue to divide uncontrollably.

3

Tumor Mass Formation

A solid tumor mass forms within one kidney (unilateral). Occasionally, it can affect both kidneys (bilateral).

4

Rapid Growth & Metastasis

The tumor grows rapidly, encapsulating itself. It can extend into the renal vein or inferior vena cava, and metastasize via blood to the lungs (most common), liver, or bones.

Clinical Manifestations

The Classic Triad

Hover or tap cards to flip.

1. Mass

Painless Abdominal Mass

Firm, smooth, deep, non-tender. Often found by a parent during bathing.

2. Hematuria

Blood in Urine

Occurs as the tumor invades the renal collecting system.

3. Hypertension

High Blood Pressure

Tumor affects renal blood flow, causing excess Renin secretion.

Gastrointestinal

  • Abdominal pain, distention.
  • Nausea, vomiting, constipation, anorexia.
  • Cause: Compression of abdominal organs by the mass.

Advanced (Metastasis)

  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath), cough, chest pain.
  • Systemic: Fever, malaise, weight loss.
  • Cause: Tumor spread via blood, most commonly to the lungs.

Diagnostic Tests

Test Purpose / Findings
Abdominal Ultrasound Detects the presence of a solid renal mass initially.
CT Scan / MRI Determines tumor size, exact location, and checks for metastasis to surrounding organs.
Chest X-ray / CT Specifically checks for lung metastasis (most common site of spread).
IV Pyelogram (IVP) Shows kidney function and shape/distortion of the renal pelvis.
Urinalysis & Bloodwork UA detects hematuria. BUN/Creatinine evaluate renal function. CBC checks overall health.
Note: Biopsy is usually deferred until post-surgery to prevent accidental puncturing and seeding of the tumor during the procedure.

Treatment & Prognosis

Surgery

Nephrectomy (removal of affected kidney and tumor) is the primary treatment. For bilateral tumors, a partial nephrectomy is attempted to preserve renal tissue.

Chemotherapy

Begins within days after diagnosis. Used pre- and post-surgery to shrink/kill residual cells.

Common Drugs: Vincristine, Dactinomycin, Doxorubicin.

Radiation

Given post-operatively once the incision heals, primarily for advanced or metastatic disease to ensure all cancer cells are destroyed.

Prognosis

Excellent if caught early (~90% survival rate).

Poor prognosis indicators:

  • Bilateral tumors.
  • Metastasis at diagnosis.
  • Unfavorable histology (anaplastic cells).

Nursing Management

Preoperative Care

  • DO NOT PALPATE ABDOMEN. Post a sign on the bed.
  • Handle the child very gently during bathing and turning.
  • Monitor Blood Pressure closely (frequently elevated due to renin).
  • Support family: A sudden pediatric cancer diagnosis causes extreme anxiety.

Postoperative Care

  • Strict I&O: Monitor urine output strictly to assess the function of the remaining single kidney.
  • Monitor for infection (surgical site, urinary tract, or due to chemo).
  • Support nutrition (high-protein, high-calorie diet for healing).
  • TEACHING: Avoid all contact sports after recovery to protect the remaining kidney.
